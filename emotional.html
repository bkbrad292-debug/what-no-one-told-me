The emotional side

When You Can’t Be the Man You’ve Always Been

Sometimes the hardest part is not the surgery, the recovery, or even the diagnosis. It’s not being able to be who you’ve always been.

The Part Nobody Really Mentions

Most men don’t exactly line up to talk about feelings.

Especially when those feelings are their own.

Who I’ve Always Been

As an adult, I’ve always been the “get up, lace your boots, and handle it” type.

That mindset started during my brief time in law enforcement, but it really solidified during two decades of being self-employed.

When you work for yourself, you learn fast:

If something needs doing, you do it.

And I did.

That work ethic became part of my identity.

It’s what I tried to model for my kids—not just preach about.

Whether it was coaching little league or taking online courses so I could learn how to renovate a house, I went all in. Every time.

That intensity?

Yeah, it didn’t land well with everyone.

Some people thought I was an asshole.

A close friend once told me:

“You’re not an asshole—you’re just intense.”

Fair enough.

Then Life Shifted

That same intensity is being tested now more than ever.

Because suddenly…

I couldn’t do the things I’ve always done while recovering.

Cutting the grass. Taking out the trash. Doing the laundry. Handling the day-to-day things around the house.

Just like that—gone.

And for someone wired like me…

That’s a brutal adjustment.

The Feeling You Don’t See Coming

There’s a word for it:

Helpless.

It’s not a feeling I’m familiar with.

It’s definitely not one I like showing.

So, like I’ve done most of my life…

I disguised it as boredom.

The Nights That Make You Face Yourself

After surgery, there were nights I barely slept.

And when you’re lying there in the dark with nothing but your thoughts…

Things get real.

Fast.

What Actually Matters

Your priorities shift.

It stops being about:

  • the newest phone
  • the car in your driveway

And becomes:

“I just had major surgery… do I still have cancer?”

Then the questions start rolling in.

The Questions That Hit Deep

Have I taught my sons what it truly means to be a man?

Not the surface-level stuff.

The real things:

  • Have I taught them to put God first?
  • To put family next?
  • To work hard?
  • To be honest?
  • To be someone others can count on?

Have I shown them how to:

  • be a good husband
  • take care of their family
  • stand firm when life gets heavy

And then it gets even more personal:

  • Have I loved my wife the way she deserves?
  • Have I let her down?

Looking Back

When you’ve been with someone since you were 17…

You build a lifetime of memories.

A lot of incredible ones.

And yeah—some failures too.

I like to believe I’ve been a good husband overall.

But I’m human.

There are moments I wish I could redo.

Thankfully, we’ve made it through all of them.

The Bigger Picture

And your mind keeps going:

  • Have I been a good son?
  • A good brother?
  • A good friend?
  • A good uncle?

I hope so.

Final Thought

Sometimes the hardest part of going through something like this…

isn’t the surgery or the recovery or even the diagnosis.

It’s this:

Not being able to be the man you’ve always been.
