What No One Told Me About Recovery at Home

Leaving the hospital felt like a win. But getting home is when the real recovery begins.

The Ride Home

After dealing with the shoulder pain, the ride home was not enjoyable. Every movement, every bump—it all made it worse.

By the time I got home, I was more than ready to sit still and try to recover.

One Thing I Highly Recommend

If there’s one thing I would strongly suggest ahead of time, it’s renting a lift chair or recliner.

Your abdominal area will be sore, and getting up from a flat position is not easy. Having something that helps lift you up makes a big difference those first few days.

The Catheter: What No One Prepares You For

Once the stronger pain meds wore off, I became very aware of the catheter.

It wasn’t necessarily painful—but it was always there. Sitting up straight was difficult, and you had to constantly think about it.

One simple tip that helped:

  • Use a clean 5-gallon bucket for the catheter bag
  • This helps prevent accidental pulling—especially if you have a dog

Getting Comfortable Is a Challenge

This was one of the biggest surprises.

The first couple of nights, I didn’t sleep in my bed. I rotated between the couch and a recliner.

Even after trying the bed again, comfort wasn’t guaranteed.

After finishing my pain meds, there were nights where I only slept 2–3 hours.

At times, my legs even had that restless feeling, which made it harder to relax.

Hygiene (Not Talked About Enough)

You’re sent home with wipes, which help—but it’s not the same as a shower.

My wife even used dry shampoo to help me feel somewhat normal.

After a couple of days, I finally showered—and it felt incredible.

Movement Matters

As uncomfortable as it can be, you need to get up and walk around.

Even a few minutes at a time helps with circulation and recovery.

Just don’t overdo it—your body needs time.

Diet and Digestion

This is something I can’t stress enough:

  • Stick to soft foods early on
  • Stay hydrated
  • Take stool softeners

Trust me—you don’t want to learn that lesson the hard way.

Increasing protein intake also helps with recovery.

Energy and Patience

Your energy levels will not be normal.

You’ll feel fine one minute and drained the next.

This is not the time to push it.

The Mental Side

There’s a mental side to recovery that doesn’t get talked about enough.

The best thing you can do is stay positive and focus on getting better.

Final Thought

Getting home feels like progress—but it’s really just the beginning.

Recovery takes time, patience, and adjusting to a new normal.

But if you take it one step at a time—you’ll get through it.
