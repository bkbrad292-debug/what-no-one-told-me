The Shoulder Pain No One Warned Me About

One of the most unexpected parts of my recovery had nothing to do with my abdomen—it was my shoulder.

It Didn’t Start That Bad

When I first woke up from surgery, things actually felt okay.

I saw my wife, Lori, my mom, and my Aunt Charlotte—all a little blurry at first as the anesthesia wore off.

As I became more alert, I made a few phone calls, sent some texts, and even cracked a few jokes.

The First Signs

Later that night, after everyone left, my nurse Katie helped me get up to walk a lap around the floor.

That first walk wasn’t bad at all.

At some point, I mentioned that my shoulders were bothering me a little.

She told me it was normal and gave me some pain medication.

The Next Morning… It Got Worse

Morning came quickly—and things changed.

The pain was now mostly in my right shoulder and it kept getting worse.

My abs were sore, but that wasn’t the problem.

The shoulder pain kept intensifying.

By Discharge… It Was Brutal

By the time I was getting ready to leave, the pain had become one of the worst I’ve experienced.

I was lying on my side, gripping the bed rail, fighting back tears.

The pain meds weren’t doing much.

The Ride Home

The ride home was miserable. Every bump made it worse.

There’s really no better way to say it—it sucked.

The Next Few Days

Once I got home, I basically lived on a heating pad for about five days.

That was the one thing that consistently helped.

Eventually, the pain went away—but those days were rough.

What’s Actually Causing This?

This type of shoulder pain is actually common after robotic or laparoscopic surgery.

It’s caused by CO₂ gas used during the procedure.

That gas can irritate the diaphragm, and because of how nerves connect, the pain gets “referred” to your shoulder.

What I Wish I Knew

When I was told it was “normal,” I didn’t expect this level of pain.

If you’re heading into surgery, here’s what I want you to know:

  • It can be intense
  • It may get worse before it gets better
  • Heat helps—a lot
  • It will go away

Final Thought

This was one of the hardest parts of early recovery—and one of the least talked about.

If you feel it, just know—you’re not alone, and it will pass.
