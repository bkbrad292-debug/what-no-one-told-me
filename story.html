I Did Everything “Right”… and Still Got Prostate Cancer

If you’re reading this, there’s a good chance you’re either concerned about your prostate health or you’ve already been diagnosed.

I want to share something that may be hard to hear—but it’s important.

I did everything “right”… and I still got prostate cancer.

Like a lot of men who try to stay ahead of their health, I wasn’t ignoring the warning signs or living recklessly. I was intentional.

I paid attention to what I put into my body. I made an effort to stay active. And when it came to prostate health specifically, I took the supplements that are commonly recommended.

And yet, I was still diagnosed.

My History With “Prostatitis” and What I Thought Was Helping

Looking back, my prostate issues didn’t start later in life—they started much earlier than I ever would have expected.

My first experience with what I was told was “prostatitis” happened when I was around 18 to 20 years old. It started as a slight burning sensation during urination.

At the time, I was prescribed antibiotics, but it never fully resolved the issue. Instead, the symptoms would come and go over the years.

One of the first things that gave me noticeable relief was a saw palmetto tea. After that, I went deeper into the world of prostate supplements and kept taking formulas with ingredients commonly associated with prostate health.

To be fair, they often seemed to help with symptoms.

But looking back, I had to ask myself whether I was truly helping my prostate—or just quieting the discomfort.

My experience taught me this: relief of symptoms is not the same as prevention.

A Note on My PSA

Looking back, there’s one detail I think is worth sharing.

Around age 50, my PSA was 1.5. It wasn’t flagged as abnormal, and technically, it fell within the standard “normal” range.

At the time, nothing more was said—and I didn’t think much of it.

But in hindsight, I wish there had at least been a conversation about watching it more closely over time.

This isn’t about blaming anyone. It’s about being an informed and engaged patient.

PSA isn’t just about one number—it’s about trends.

And part of that is establishing a baseline.

A single number might fall within a “normal” range, but without tracking it over time, it’s hard to know how quickly things may be changing.

Looking back, I think having that conversation—about establishing a baseline and monitoring changes year to year—would have been valuable.

The Biopsy: What I Wasn’t Prepared For

At some point, everything becomes real—and for me, that moment was the biopsy.

What I wasn’t fully prepared for was what came after. Once the medication began to wear off, I experienced a level of discomfort that caught me off guard.

For a couple of hours, the pain was intense. Eventually, my body basically shut down and I passed out. When I woke up, the pain was gone.

Since then, I’ve talked to a couple of other men who had similar experiences. It’s not something that gets talked about enough.

The Diagnosis: The Moment Everything Changed

Like most things these days, the news didn’t come from a phone call—it came from an email.

I got the notification that my biopsy results were available. I logged in expecting good news. After all, I had done everything “right.”

But as I started reading, I got the shock of my life:

Cancer.

Gleason 3+4=7. Then came another detail that changed everything: cribriform carcinoma.

I walked upstairs and told my wife. From there came the texts, the calls, the family conversations, and the reality that life had just changed.

Choosing Surgery

I spent a lot of time researching surgery, radiation, outcomes, and side effects.

I ultimately leaned toward surgery because I would rather deal with the side effects up front and do everything I could to recover from them than face a different kind of uncertainty later.

Meeting with my surgeon helped solidify that decision.

The Morning of Surgery

The morning of surgery, reality finally hit me.

I had accidentally put creamer in my coffee out of habit, and while lying in pre-op I realized how serious all of this was. I was about to go under for hours. This was real.

Once I Got Home: The Reality of Recovery

Leaving the hospital felt like a win, but getting home is when the real recovery starts.

Getting comfortable was much harder than I expected. Sleep was inconsistent, movement took effort, and the catheter changed everything for a while.

A lift chair helped. Walking around the house helped. Staying hydrated, eating soft foods, and taking stool softeners helped.

Recovery at home is where the real work begins.

The First Post-Surgery Appointment

This appointment brought two major things: catheter removal and final pathology.

Getting the catheter removed was a relief. Then came the final pathology—and a gut punch.

The surgeon told me that we 100% made the correct choice by having surgery. I was upgraded from Gleason 3+4=7 (Stage 2) to Gleason 4+3=7 (Stage 3, T3a), meaning the cancer had started to extend beyond the prostate and was beginning to break through the outer wall.

The Waiting Game… Again

Follow-up scans and PSA testing were supposed to bring clarity.

Instead, they brought another surprise: a Bosniak 4 cyst on my right kidney.

At that point, I had to process the fact that I might be dealing with another cancer entirely. It was another gut punch and another return to uncertainty.

A Question I Asked Myself

One thing I’ve thought about a lot is whether anything I did after diagnosis made things worse.

I cut back on alcohol, but I didn’t completely stop. And like a lot of people, I wondered whether that somehow contributed to the cancer being worse than originally thought.

From everything I’ve learned, the more likely explanation is that the cancer was already there and the post-surgery pathology simply gave a more complete picture.

But that kind of second-guessing is real, and I think it’s worth acknowledging.

Why I’m Sharing This

I’m sharing this because there is a huge gap between medical information and lived experience.

There are things doctors explain well. And then there are things you only really understand once you’ve lived them.

My goal is simple: to share the information I wish I had, in plain language, without sugarcoating it.
